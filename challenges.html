Key Challenges: Reliability, Citation, Responsibility

Hallucination Problem

Research has found that there are 58% to 88% of hallucination rates for legal queries in response to specific legal questions, which are often the legal issues lawyers are asked to address. Even the most advanced LLMs produce almost 30% of incorrect information on high skate legal tasks. The problem is even accelerating that a database tracking AL hallucination in court case recorded 36 instances in 2024, but 48 instance in just the first half of 2025.

Real Word Consequences

Mata v. Avianca (2023): A law firm submitted a brief containing fake case citations generated by ChatGPT. The judge described the submission as “utterly and unusually fraudulent”.

Ayinde v. London Borough of Haringey (2025): A trainee barrister cited a case that did not exist at all, the High Court ruled that “freely available generative AI tools are not capable of conducting reliable legal research”.

Citation Problems Beyond Hallucination

Misattribution: Occur when the model correctly identifies a real case, but it assigns the case to the wrong holding, mistakes the facts or misrepresents what the court actually decided.

Citation drift: Occur when citations are copied and pasted repeatedly across document, this distorts the connection between the citation and the original document.

Jurisdictional confusion: Occur when LLMs mic legal rules from different jurisdictions without indicating which rule is applied.

Responsibility and Liability

The lawyer should remain fully responsible for all AI generated content, while using AI does not mitigate professional liability in any way. Courts have rejected the argument that AI errors could be attributed to negligence or unintentional mistakes. The Ayinde court ruling stated that “Lawyers, junior or senior, are fully responsible for what they put before the court. Up to May 2025, according to the Law Society “Generate AI: The Essentials”, AI literacy is being considered a baseline professional competence.
